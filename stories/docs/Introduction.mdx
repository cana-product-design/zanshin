# 残心

<p style={{fontSize: '32px', fontWeight: 300, lineHeight: 1.3, color: '#1C1C1E', margin: '0 0 64px 0', letterSpacing: '-0.01em'}}>Lingering awareness.<br />The moment after action when the mind stays fully present.</p>

---

## Where it began

Kendo taught me something that UX workshops never did.

In the dojo, *zanshin* names the posture you hold after a strike. The sword has landed. The match may be over. But the kendōka stays — eyes open, weight balanced, attention unbroken. The body doesn't celebrate or collapse. It remains.

I practiced kendo for three years while working as a product designer. Every evening I would leave a screen full of components and enter a space that had none: bare wood floors, white walls, the smell of cotton and sweat. The contrast was difficult to ignore. Digital interfaces move frantically — hover states snap, modals flash in, alerts compete for attention. The dojo moved at a completely different speed.

I started asking: what would a UI feel like if it understood zanshin?

## The dye vat

Aizome is the traditional Japanese craft of indigo dyeing. Artisans in Tokushima province have practised it for centuries using fermented indigo — a living dye bath that reacts differently each day depending on temperature, humidity, and the hands working it.

The characteristic colors of aizome cloth — deep navy (*kachiiro*, 勝色, the "color of victory"), midnight indigo (*nōkon*, 濃紺), pale sky (*asagi*, 浅葱) — are not applied. They are absorbed. The fabric goes into the vat and comes out changed. You repeat the immersion twenty, thirty, forty times to build depth.

Those blues became the palette for the disciplined mode of this system. Not because they look good (they do), but because the process behind them is the metaphor: restraint, repetition, depth built slowly. Nothing instant. Nothing decorative.

The Aizome palette carries this spirit. Every color was chosen because it holds its ground under scrutiny — tested against APCA thresholds, not just WCAG, because accessibility in this system is not a checkbox. It is part of the craft.

## The kintsugi side

Alongside kendo I kept a portfolio site — [canabrava.me](https://www.canabrava.me) — where I documented work and thinking in a completely different register.

That site is warm. It uses Cormorant Garamond at weight 300, which is barely ink on the page. The background is shironeri (白練), an off-white named for bleached silk — not pure white, not gray, something between. There are no shadows. No dark mode. The accent, when it appears at all, is the orange-red of a hanko seal pressed in wax.

The guiding principle there is *yohaku-no-bi* — the beauty of what is left out. A design that withholds a little always reveals more than one that gives everything at once.

The Kintsugi palette in this system comes from that sensibility. Gold where the portfolio uses hanko red, but the same restraint: one accent, used sparingly, applied only where it earns its place.

## One system, two disciplines

Most design systems have a single personality. Zanshin has two because the work I do has two moods.

**Kintsugi** is the editorial mode: warm washi surfaces, gold as the single accent, Cormorant Garamond for display. It's for portfolios, documentation, long-form content — anywhere the reader should feel invited to dwell.

**Aizome** is the product mode: kachiiro grounds, indigo and asagi accents, Inter for legibility under cognitive load. It's for dashboards, SaaS UIs, developer tools — anywhere the user needs clarity over contemplation.

Both modes share a common foundation: the same spacing scale, the same border-radius ceiling (8px — nothing rounder than a folded paper corner), the same timing floor (300ms — nothing faster than a slow breath), the same APCA-validated token pairs. Switch the palette at runtime with a single prop. The structure stays constant; only the feeling shifts.

## What you're holding

This is not a component library that happens to look Japanese. The components encode the philosophy:

- The **Divider** fades to nothing at both ends — ink absorbed into washi
- The **Button** drops `1px` on press and holds there briefly — zanshin after action
- The **Nav** underline expands with a long ease, then stays — the mark of presence
- The **Card** has no shadow in kintsugi mode — the surface itself carries the weight
- Focus rings use gold in kintsugi, indigo in aizome — always visible, never decorative
- `prefers-reduced-motion` silences all transitions — because stillness is also part of the aesthetic

Every one of these decisions is documented. Every color pair has an Lc value. Every token has a name that refers to something real.

---

## The name

Zanshin (残心) is written with two kanji: *zan* (残, "remaining") and *shin* (心, "heart/mind"). Literally: the remaining mind.

It is the quality of attention that doesn't stop at completion. After the sword stroke, after the bow, after shipping the feature — the mind stays present, noticing what the action revealed.

That is the aspiration for every interface built with this system.
